TITLE:
Compliance-First Data Processing Architecture: How Regulatory Convergence Shapes Processing Engine Architecture

AUTHOR:
Daniel Berry, Signature Strategy

ABSTRACT:
Organizations that process data under regulatory obligation face architectural demands from multiple, independently developed legal and standards frameworks. This paper examines five such frameworks: civil litigation discovery rules (Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Federal Rules of Evidence, Daubert reliability standard), digital forensics standards (ISO 27037, ISO 27042, SWGDE best practices), federal records management requirements (44 U.S.C. Chapter 31, NARA General Records Schedules, OMB Circular A-130), financial services compliance obligations (SEC Rule 17a-4, FINRA Rules 3110 and 4511, SOX Section 802), and data privacy regulations (GDPR Articles 5, 17, 22, and 25, with reference to CCPA and emerging U.S. state privacy laws).

Despite independent origins, these five traditions converge on nine identical architectural demands: immutable audit trails, cryptographic integrity verification, chain of custody documentation, complete processing logs, dual-mode processing capability, independent verification, error detection with quantified tolerance thresholds, reproducible outputs, and algorithmic decision transparency. The paper presents the regulatory basis for each demand, demonstrates convergence through cross-tradition analysis, and derives nine defensible architectural principles that any compliant processing system should satisfy.

The framework is presented from the perspective of a systems integrator working across multiple regulated sectors. It is intended as a practical architectural reference for engineers, compliance officers, and legal technologists designing or evaluating data processing systems that must withstand regulatory scrutiny.
